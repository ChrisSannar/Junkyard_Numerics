# Glossary — Originalism Searcher

Terms we use with precise meaning in this project. When code, docs, or prompts use one of these words, it means exactly this.

## Legal / domain terms

**Original public meaning** — What the words of a legal text meant to an ordinary, competent reader at the time of ratification (here: 1876). A *semantic* claim about language, evidenced by contemporaneous usage — dictionaries, newspapers, debates. NOT the drafters' intent, and NOT public opinion about the provision.

**Public sentiment** — What people *felt and argued* about a provision at the time: editorials, speeches, political controversy. An *attitudinal* claim. Persuasive context, but weaker as legal evidence than semantic-usage evidence. Kept in a separate, labeled output section — never blended into meaning claims.

**History and tradition** — The record of laws, regulation, enforcement, and practice concerning a subject *over time* (ratification era → present-ish), as demanded by *N.Y. State Rifle & Pistol Ass'n v. Bruen* (2022) and *Dobbs* (2022) style analysis. A *practice* claim, distinct from both meaning and sentiment.

**Provision** — A discrete unit of the 1876 Texas Constitution: an article + section (e.g., Art. I, § 13). The primary key of the whole system.

**Pinpoint citation** — A citation that resolves to a specific, viewable location in a primary source: newspaper name + date + page (+ column if possible), or journal page number. Every factual claim in an output memo must carry one.

**Concordance line** — A short excerpt showing a target word/phrase in its surrounding context (KWIC: keyword-in-context). The atomic unit of semantic-meaning evidence.

**Ratification era** — For this project: roughly 1874–1878 (convention called 1875, constitution ratified Feb 1876), the window in which contemporaneous usage evidence counts toward original public meaning.

## Sources (proper nouns)

**Convention Journal** — *Journal of the Constitutional Convention of the State of Texas* (1875), the official record of proceedings that drafted the 1876 constitution.

**McKay Debates** — Seth Shepard McKay's compilation *Debates in the Texas Constitutional Convention of 1875*, reconstructed from newspaper reports (published 1930).

**Portal** — The Portal to Texas History (UNT Libraries), digitized Texas newspaper and document archive.

**Chronicling America** — Library of Congress digitized newspaper archive with a public JSON API.

**Gammel's** — *Gammel's Laws of Texas*, the standard compilation of early Texas statutes.

## Product terms

**Research memo** — The output artifact: a structured memo for an appellate lawyer where every claim links to a pinpoint-cited, viewable primary source. The lawyer verifies and lifts; the tool never files anything.

**Evidence section** — One of the three labeled parts of a memo: (1) Semantic meaning, (2) History & tradition, (3) Sentiment context. The three are never merged into a single narrative.

**Deep provision** — One of the 1–2 provisions we build the full, presentation-grade evidence pipeline for. Other provisions get a thinner, uniform treatment.
